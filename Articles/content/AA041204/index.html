The concerning air quality in Jakarta, Indonesia




The air quality in Jakarta is a pressing concern, with millions of residents affected by severe pollution. In Jakarta, a city with over 11 million people, the average resident’s life expectancy would be 5.5 years less if the current situation remains unchanged.


Meanwhile, in Jakarta, which is one of the most populous cities in Indonesia, one of the contributors to air pollution is motor vehicle fumes. Not only from vehicle exhaust, factories also produce gas from chimneys which also has the potential to contribute to pollutants. Eight coal-fired power plants operate within 100 kilometers of Jakarta, emitting hazardous pollutants in addition, the weather conditions during the dry season, with low rainfall intensity.
Source : Kompas


The air pollution in Jakarta is influenced by several factors, including local sources within the city and contributions from nearby regions.

• Tanggerang is part of the Greater Jakarta metropolitan area, It hosts industrial zones, factories, and power plants that emit pollutants.
• Bekasi is situated to the east of Jakarta, is another significant air pollution contributor from vehicles, factories, and construction sites in.
• Satellite based analysis reveals that emissions in West Java like Bandung, have been increasing over the years. These emmisions contributes alot to Jakarta's pollution levels.
Source : energyandcleanair


Now, Jakarta isn't the worst city to live in, it's not dangerous. But as the heroes of our planet, one small action can make this world a healthy place for everybody. So here's some strategies to help mitigate air pollution
• Implementing vehicle emissions tests ensures that vehicles comply with emission standards. This helps reduce harmful pollutants released into the air.
• Develop and promote eco-friendly public transportation options. Investing in busses, trains, and other low-emmision modes of transport. Making more and more highway isn't going to work.
• Expand areas that are free from motorized vehicles. Creating car-free zones in certain parts of the city can reduce traffic congestion and emissions.
