How Flying with an Airplane Contribute to Climate Change




Did you know, that aviation actually contribute to climate change?

Airplanes emit 100 times more CO₂ per hour than a shared bus, or even a train ride. Researchers calculated that aviation contributes around 4% to global warming, and is projected to cause about 0.1° Celsius (0.2° Fahrenheit) of warming by 2050 if aviation continues growing at pre-pandemic rates. When jet fuel burns, it produces both CO₂ and non-CO₂ emission, including nitrogen oxides, soot, water vapor, and sulfate aerosols. any CO₂ you emit from a jet engine or your car, will stays in the air.
Source : Mongabay



So, should we give up flying to protect our planet from those bad CO₂?

For those who fly, it is likely to make up a significant slice of our personal carbon footprint. This is because, mile for mile, flying is the most damaging way to travel for the climate. A return flight from London to San Francisco emits around 5.5 tonnes of CO₂, that is more than twice the emmision produced by a family car in a year!
Source : BBC


Here are some actions that you can do
• Opt for alternative modes of transportation, such as trains or busses.
• Fly using an airplane less frequently. Try to prioritize essential trips and explore local destination.
• Take direct flight when possible, connecting flight consumes more addition fuels.


But there's actually some "good news" for the aviation world. Rolls-Royce announced the successful test of an aircraft engine that runs on hydrogen. The British company built the engine using a converted Rolls-Royce AE 2100-A with the airline easyJet to explore replacing carbon-intensive fuels used in aviation. “The success of this hydrogen test is an exciting milestone,” said Rolls-Royce’s chief technology officer Grazia Vittadini in a statement.
